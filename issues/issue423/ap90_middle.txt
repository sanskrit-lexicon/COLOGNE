<H>{@{|APPENDIX I.|}@}
{|SANSKRIT PROSODY.|}
<H>{%Introduction%}.
<HI>The earliest and most important work in Sanskrit <lbinfo n="pro+sody"/>
prosody is the Piṅgala-chhandas-śāstra, attributed
to the sage Piṅgala, which consists of Sūtras <lbinfo n="dis+tributed"/>
distributed over eight books. The Agni Purāṇa also
gives a complete system of prosody founded <lbinfo n="ap+parently"/>
apparently on Piṅgala's. Several other original
treatises have likewise been composed by various
authors, such as the Śrutabodha, Vāṇībhūṣaṇa,
Vṛtta-darpaṇa, Vṛtta-ratnākara, Vṛtta-kaumudī,
Chandomañjarī &c. In the following pages the
Chandomañjarī and Vṛtta-ratnākara have been
chiefly drawn upon, Vedic as well as Prākṛta
metres have been ignored in this Appendix.
<HI>Sanskrit composition may be in the form of {#gadya#}
‘prose’ or {#padya#} ‘verse’ or poetry expressed in the
form of stanzas.
<HI>A stanza or {%padya%} is a combination of four {%pādas%} or
quarters, which are regulated either by the number
of syllables ({#akzara#}), or by the number of syllabic
instants ({#mAtrA#}).
<HI>A {#padya#} is a {#vftta#} or {#jAti#}.
<HI>A {#vftta#} is a stanza the metre of which is regulated by
the number and position of syllables in each Pāda
or quarter. A {#jAti#} is a stanza the metre of which
is regulated by the number of syllabic instants in
each quarter.
<HI>Vṛttas are divided into three classes: {#--samavftta#} in
which the Pādas or quarters composing the stanza
are all similar; {#arDasamavftta#} in which the alternate
quarters are similar; and {#vizaMmavftta#} in which the
quarters are all dissimilar.
<HI>A syllable is as much of a word as can be pronounced
at once, that is, a vowel with or without one or
more consonants.
<HI>A syllable is {#laGu#} ‘short’ or {#guru#} ‘long’, according as
its vowel is ‘short’ or ‘long’. The vowels {#a, i, u#},
{#f#}, and {#x#} short; and {#A, I, U, f, e, E, o#}, and 
[Page1179-b+ 40]
{#O#} are long. But a short vowel becomes long in
prosody when it is followed by an {%Anusvāra%} or
{%Visarga%}, or by a conjunct consonant; as the vowel {#a#}
in {#gaMDa#} or {#gaH#}. (The consonants {#pra#} & {#Ta#}, as also {#bra#}, & {#kra#},
are said to be exceptions, before which the vowel
may be short by a sort of poetical license; e. g.
in Ku. 7. 11. or Śi. 10. 60, where, however, <lbinfo n="emen+dations"/>
emendations have been proposed by critics to render the
metre comformable to the general laws of prosody).
So also the last syllable of a {%pāda%} is either long or
short, according to the exigence of the metre, <lbinfo n="what+ever"/>
whatever be its natural length.
{#sAnusvAraSca dIrGa Sca visargI ca gururBavet .#}
{#varRaH saMyogapUrvaSca taTA pAdAMtago'pi vA ..#}
<HI>In metres regulated by the number of syllabic instants,
one instant or Mātrā is allotted to a short vowel,
and two to a long one.
<HI>For the purpose of scanning metres regulated by the
number of syllables, writers on prosody have <lbinfo n="de+vised"/>
devised eight ‘Gaṇas’ or syllabic feet, each <lbinfo n="consist+ing"/>
consisting of three syllables, and distinguished from one
another by particular syllables being short or long.
They are given in the following verse:
--{#mastrigurustrilaGuSca nakAro#}
{#BAdiguruH punarAdilaGuryaH .#}
{#jo gurumaDyagato ralamaDyaH#}
{#soM'taguruH kaTitoM'talaGustaH ..#}
{#AdimaDyAvasAnezu yaratA yAMti lAGavam .#}
{#BajasA gOravaM yAMti manO tu gurulAGavam ..#}
<HI>Expressed in symbols (the symbol ˘ denoting a
short syllable, and ¯ a long one), the different
Gaṇas may be represented as follows:--
{#ya#} ˘ ¯ ¯ (Bacchius)
{#ra#} ¯ ˘ ¯ (Amphimacer)
{#ta#} ¯ ¯ ˘ (Anti-bacchius)
{#Ba#} ¯ ˘ ˘ (Dactylus)
{#ja#} ˘ ¯ ˘ (Amphibrachys)
{#sa#} ˘ ˘ ¯ (Anapæstus)
{#ma#} ¯ ¯ ¯ (Molossus)
{#na#} ˘ ˘ ˘ (Tribrachys)
[Page1180-a+ 50]
Similarly {#la#} (˘) is used to denote a short syllable,
and {#ga#} (¯) a long one.
<HI>{%N. B%}. --Sanskrit prosodists classify Vṛttas according
to the number of syllables contained in each <lbinfo n="quar+ter"/>
quarter. Thus they enumerate twenty-six classes of
‘Samavṛttas’, as the number of syllables in each
quarter of a regular metre may vary from one to
twenty-six. Each of these classes comprehends a
great number of possible metres according to the
different modes in which long and short syllables
may be distributed. For example, in the class
where each quarter contains six syllables, each of
the six syllables may be either short or long, and
thus the number of {%possible%} combinations is
2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 or 2^6 = 64, though not even half
a dozen are in general use; so in the case of the
twenty-six syllabled class, the possible varieties are
2^26 or 87, 108, 864! But if we consider the cases
where the alternate quarters are similar or all <lbinfo n="dis+similar"/>
dissimilar, the variety of possible metres is almost <lbinfo n="in+finite"/>
infinite. Piṅgala, as also the Līlāvatī and the last
chapter of Vṛtta-ratnākara, give directions for
computing the number of possible varieties and for
finding their places, or that of any single one, in a
regular enumeration of them. The different <lbinfo n="varie+ties"/>
varieties, however, which have been used by poets are
few when compared with the vast multitude of
possible metres. But even these are too many to be
dealt with in an Appendix like this, and we shall,
therefore, only give such kinds as are most <lbinfo n="frequ+ently"/>
frequently employed or require particular notice, in the
following order:--
{%Section%} A {#samavftta#}
{%Section%} B {#arDavftta#}
{%Section%} C {#vizamavftta#}
{%Section%} D {#jAti#} &c.

will often be found to have dropped their vowels
for the exigence of metre. {%<ab>e. g.</ab>%} {#mra Bna#} stands for {#ma#},
{#ra, ma, na#}; so {#mto#}, for {#ma, ta#} &c. The first line gives the
{%Definition%} of a metre; the second, the {%Scheme in%}
{%Gaṇas%} with the {%Yati%} or Cæsura - the pause that
may be made in reciting a quarter or verse, and
which is usually indicated by the words in the <lbinfo n="De+finition"/>
Definition standing in the Instrumental case-denoted
in brackets by Arabic figures, and then comes the
example (many of these examples are drawn from
the works of Māgha, Bhāravi, Kālidāsa, Daṇḍin
&c.).

<H>({%d%}) {#mAtrAsamaka#}
<P>The metre called {#mAtrAsamaka#} consists of four quarters
each of which contains 16 syllabic instants. The
most general variety is that in which the ninth <lbinfo n="sylla+bic"/>
syllabic instant is composed of a short syllable, and the
last is a long syllable. It is defined as {#mAtrAsamakaM navamo#}
{#lgAMtyaH .#}
<P>But there are several varieties of this metre
arising from particular syllabic instants being short or
long. For example, if the 9th and 12th moments are
formed by short syllables, and the 15th and 16th by
a long one, and the rest are optional, it is called
{#vAnavAsikA#}. If the 5th, 8th, and 9th are formed by
short syllables, and the 15th and 16th by a long one,
it is called {#citrA#}. If the 5th and 8th are short, as
also the 9th and 10th, and 15th and 16th are long'
it is called {#upacitrA#}. And if the 5th, 8th, and 12th
are short, 15th and 16th long, and the rest <lbinfo n="indetermi+nate"/>
indeterminate, it is called {#viSloka#}. Sometimes two or more of
these varieties are combined in the same stanza, and
in that case the measure is called {#pAdAkulaka#}, in which
there is no other restriction than that each quarter
should have sixteen syllabic instants.
